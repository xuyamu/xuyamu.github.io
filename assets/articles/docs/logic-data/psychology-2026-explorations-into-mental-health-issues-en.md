**"Electronic Impotence" and Brain Stimulus Tolerance**

*Contented as the Emperor, yet yearning for immortality. 
— Zhu Zaiyu (Ming Dynasty), "Song of Insatiability"*

　　Netizens who frequently play video games may have encountered or heard of a phenomenon known as "electronic impotence" (often referred to as electronic ED). The term "electronic impotence" describes "a phenomenon of losing interest and lacking enthusiasm for digital entertainment such as video games, mobile phones, and short videos."

　　The neural basis of the "electronic impotence" phenomenon is the brain's symptom of developing tolerance or desensitization to stimuli. After long-term exposure to high-intensity stimuli from electronic products (such as games and short videos), the brain's reward system (primarily the dopamine pathway) undergoes neuroadaptation, leading to a decrease in sensitivity to the original stimuli, which requires stronger or more stimuli to produce the same sense of pleasure.

　　An individual's development of tolerance or desensitization to stimuli is commonly seen in the process of addiction development. Addiction is typically divided into two categories: substance addiction and behavioral addiction. The *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition, Text Revision* (DSM-5-TR) defines Substance Use Disorder (SUD) as a behavioral pattern caused by substance use that results in clinically significant impairment or distress. This disorder includes 11 diagnostic criteria; meeting at least 2 within a 12-month period warrants a diagnosis, which is categorized as mild, moderate, or severe based on the number of symptoms. Its core features include: loss of control over use, increased tolerance, withdrawal symptoms, compulsive use, and continued use despite adverse consequences. Persistent changes in the brain's reward system (especially the dopamine pathway) are a crucial mechanism [1]. The *International Classification of Diseases, 11th Revision* (ICD-11) released by the World Health Organization (WHO) establishes a subcategory of "Disorders due to addictive behaviours" under the broader category of "Disorders due to substance use or addictive behaviours," which includes Gambling Disorder and Gaming Disorder. Other behaviors that may possess addictive characteristics (such as shopping, sexual behavior, eating, etc.) can be classified under "Other specified disorders due to addictive behaviours" or related categories. This classification emphasizes that repetitive and rewarding behaviors can lead to impaired control, increased priority given to the behavior, and continued negative consequences [2].

　　How do the symptoms of the brain's stimulus tolerance form? During the neural activity underlying the development of tolerance, the reduction of dopamine receptors and the accumulation of ΔFosB are two key factors triggering tolerance, which have been extensively studied in addiction reward models and chronic psychostimulant exposure. Dopamine receptors are molecular "receivers" distributed on the membranes of nerve cells. By accurately capturing dopamine signals, they convert chemical information into physiological responses that regulate human motor control, emotional reward, and cognitive functions. ΔFosB (DeltaFosB) is a transcription factor found in brain neurons.

　　The ICSS (Intracranial Self-Stimulation) model is a classic behavioral pharmacological model in neuroscience and addiction research, used to study the function, mechanism, and drug/stimulus effects on the brain's reward system (especially the dopamine pathway). The experiment operates like this: electrodes are implanted in specific reward areas of the brain in an animal (usually a rat or mouse), and the animal self-administers brief electrical stimulation (usually 0.2–0.5 seconds) by lever-pressing. The ICSS threshold reflects the brain's sensitivity to "reward" or "pleasure"; a decreased threshold equals increased sensitivity, while an increased threshold equals decreased sensitivity.

　　Studies have found that in rats exposed to cocaine for long periods, their ICSS threshold consistently increases and cannot return to baseline, proving a chronic alteration in the regulatory function of the brain's reward system. After prolonged exposure to cocaine, the baseline threshold permanently shifts upward, leaving the rats in an "anhedonic" state. This forces them to consume more drugs to offset this negative state, thereby leading to a continuous escalation in intake [3].

　　Research shows that cocaine abusers, alcohol abusers, and heroin abusers exhibit reduced availability of dopamine D2 receptors. The reason for this reduced availability is that the acute pharmacological action of methamphetamine leads to an increase in extracellular dopamine concentration, which in turn causes receptor downregulation [4]. Mouse experiments indicate that chronic cocaine self-administration decreases the sensitivity (rather than purely the density) of D2 receptors in the nucleus accumbens (NAc). This is achieved by altering G protein subunit (Gαo) coupling. This cocaine-induced reduction in D2R sensitivity promotes the production of cocaine's rewarding effects (increasing the probability of the animal repeatedly seeking/using it and generating subjective pleasure) [5].

　　Why does addiction continue to affect mental health? At the molecular level, addictive behavior is related to the accumulation of ΔFosB. The two main transcriptional regulators known to change after repeated drug exposure are CREB and ΔFosB [6]. When the brain is stimulated (such as by ingesting drugs of abuse or experiencing intense rewards), neurons rapidly synthesize a class of proteins called "immediate early genes" (IEGs), such as c-Fos and full-length FosB. These proteins act like alarms; they spike quickly but are degraded and return to baseline levels within a few hours [7]. However, ΔFosB has a very long half-life, which allows drug-induced changes in gene expression to persist for a long time even after drug use stops. Therefore, addiction can be viewed as a long-term neural plasticity disorder [6].

　　In other words, repeated stimulation of the dopamine system leads to a decrease in D2 receptor (DRD2) density or sensitivity alongside the accumulation of ΔFosB. This is an important cause of reward tolerance, manifesting as the need for higher stimulus intensity to produce the same rewarding effect, and this tolerance can persist for a long time.

　　The most common usage of "electronic impotence" is to describe a state of losing interest in games. Although Gaming Disorder has been listed as a "Disorder due to addictive behaviours" by the ICD-11, "electronic impotence" is not considered a disease because it does not fully possess addictive characteristics (mere tolerance does not meet diagnostic criteria).

　　Short videos are a widely used digital entertainment product today, and they have profoundly changed the daily lives of modern people. The number of short video users is massive. Research shows that individuals addicted to short videos have a higher tendency for risky decision-making and shorter reaction times. The rapid and massive release of dopamine alters brain reward processing, leading to increased impulsivity and pleasure-seeking [8].

　　The emergence of blockchain technology has also birthed a new type of behavioral addiction: cryptocurrency trading addiction. When a person acquires immense wealth through cryptocurrency investments, their dopamine is released in massive amounts instantly, which in turn causes receptor downregulation and ΔFosB accumulation. Regardless of whether a heavily involved crypto speculator ultimately succeeds in achieving financial freedom through crypto investments or suffers heavy losses, once they experience drastic surges and plunges in their assets, their neural structure is highly susceptible to being affected. Cases of people becoming helplessly addicted to crypto trading, leading to property loss, impaired physical and mental health, or drastically altered life trajectories, are common. Bilibili influencer "Motorcycle Driver Master Xu(摩的司机徐师傅)" recounted such a tragic story of crypto addiction in a video titled "Xiamen University Student's Life Collapses After Becoming Obsessed with Crypto: Dropping Out, Running Away, Girlfriend Leaving, and Heavily in Debt."(https://www.bilibili.com/video/BV1DDCFB8Ezv)

　　TRON founder Justin Sun was publicly accused on social media by his ex-girlfriend Zeng Ying of breaking up with her on the grounds of having "different social statuses." After acquiring massive wealth through cryptocurrency, Sun frequently interacted with celebrities and politicians and engaged in marketing hype. These behaviors inevitably increased his reward threshold, which might have resulted in him no longer being able to derive pleasure from Zeng Ying, ultimately leading to their breakup.

　　For ordinary cryptocurrency investors, what they pursue is financial freedom, while for industry leaders who have already achieved financial freedom, they pursue higher-status partners or luxury items like immortality. I suggest that crypto leaders pay attention to their own physical and mental health, as well as that of others within their circle.

　　Based on my observations, the desire for one's own physical immortality may stem from the following psychological mechanisms:  
　　1. Fear of death. Some people have profound experiences with and fears of death, and sometimes this fear stems from past experiences with illness.

　　2. The belief that a finite life cannot satisfy their infinite desires. These desires include the thirst for power, knowledge, or material enjoyment. (The infinite thirst for knowledge is commonly seen in immensely curious and erudite intellectuals.)

　　The China National Committee for Terms in Sciences and Technologies defines "disease" as: "The process of abnormal life activities occurring due to disrupted homeostatic regulation under the damaging effects of a certain etiology. During this process, the body experiences abnormal changes in function, metabolism, and morphological structure, resulting in various symptoms, signs, and behavioral abnormalities caused by an imbalance with the internal and external environments [9]." In other words, a disease is first and foremost an abnormal life activity. What is an "abnormal life activity"? Can "dopamine tolerance" be considered an "abnormal life activity"? In every era, the public's definition and understanding of "abnormal" differs. The phenomena of human sacrifice and funerary human sacrifice were widespread in history; today, human sacrifice and funerary human sacrifice are considered abnormal, though the Shang Dynasty people and the Aztecs might have held different views on this.

　　Today, aging is also not considered a disease. Is aging an "abnormal life activity"? Aging-related diseases (cardiovascular diseases like hypertension and coronary heart disease, neurodegenerative diseases like Alzheimer's and Parkinson's, and metabolic/endocrine diseases like type 2 diabetes) are viewed as diseases, so why is aging itself not considered a disease? Viewing "aging" as a natural physiological process is a historical convention, and treating "aging" as a disease also faces a series of practical issues—including the fact that we do not yet have enough medical resources to cope with the ubiquitous phenomenon of aging.

　　Although our medical system does not define "dopamine tolerance" and "aging" as diseases and has not developed corresponding treatments for them, "dopamine tolerance" and "aging" undeniably bring negative emotions and suffering to people. To what extent should we exert effort to help humanity break free from "dopamine tolerance" and "aging"?

**Impact of Generative AI on the Dopamine System**

*In the past, the days grew slowly; carriages, horses, and mail were all slow. One’s entire life was only enough to love one person. 
— Mu Xin, "In the Past, Everything Was Slow"*

　　One of the core functions of dopamine is processing Reward Prediction Error (RPE). Simply put, the release of dopamine does not depend on the amount of reward an individual receives, but rather on whether the received reward exceeds expectations [10]. The mesolimbic dopaminergic pathway is a core pathway in the brain's reward and motivation system, primarily consisting of dopaminergic neurons projecting from the ventral tegmental area (VTA) in the midbrain to the nucleus accumbens (NAc, which is part of the ventral striatum) [11]. When an individual forms an expectation or mental representation of a positive outcome (such as a potential reward or goal achievement), VTA dopamine neurons are activated, releasing dopamine into the NAc. This endows the relevant cues with incentive salience, generating a motivational drive of "wanting" or deeming it "worth pursuing"[12].
　
　Generative AI has lowered the barrier for users to obtain large amounts of dopamine. For those seeking emotional support, rapid and low-cost emotional support can be obtained by chatting with Large Language Models (LLMs). For knowledge seekers, the barrier to acquiring knowledge through interaction with LLMs is also very low. AI can provide information of interest cheaply and quickly, which may lead to increasingly higher expectations for things the user finds interesting. The vast amount of visual information generated by image and video models can also easily lead to aesthetic fatigue due to tolerance. In real life, sources of pleasing visual information—such as high-attractiveness humans or beautiful scenery—are rare. However, image and video generation models can produce such content with an extremely low threshold. The flooding of these images and videos accelerates the development of tolerance. The human brain is not adapted to an environment suddenly flooded with high-value visual information created by AI.

　　Additionally, adult content (text, images, and videos) generated by generative AI is another major source of accelerated tolerance. Even before the era of generative AI, the impact of pornographic content on the reward system had been studied. Research shows that more time spent watching pornography per week is associated with smaller gray matter volume in the right caudate (part of the reward system); decreased activation of the left putamen when viewing sexual stimuli; and weakened functional connectivity between the right caudate and the prefrontal cortex. Intensely stimulating the reward system leads to neuroplastic changes, similar to desensitization in addiction [13]. The difference from watching adult content produced by others is that content generated by generative AI is user-customized and aligns more closely with the user's specific tastes, which may speed up the rate of tolerance.

　　Our genes are unable to adapt to modern social life, a phenomenon known in evolutionary biology as "evolutionary mismatch." Obesity and diabetes are frequently discussed examples of evolutionary mismatch. The abuse of dopamine pathways and reward systems is also a classic case. The half-life of our ΔFosB cannot adapt to the speed at which current digital entertainment products and generative AI raise our reward thresholds. In primitive societies, high-value rewards (such as discovering a sugar-rich fruit grove, a successful hunt, or reproductive opportunities) were very scarce. The slow accumulation and long half-life of ΔFosB acted as a kind of "long-term memory and motivational locking mechanism" for the brain. It allowed early humans to maintain a strong desire and sustained motivation for those scarce survival resources even through long treks and countless failures.


**Changes in Human Value and Mental Health Issues in the Era of Artificial Intelligence**

*"More human than human" is our motto.*
*— Eldon Tyrell, Blade Runner*

　　Unlike other factors that cause behavioral addiction, such as gambling, short videos, games, and social media, generative AI like large language models can, to a certain extent, replace the emotional value provided by humans.

　　Theory of Mind refers to the cognitive ability of an individual to attribute mental states (such as beliefs, intentions, desires, emotions, knowledge, etc.) to oneself and others, and to understand that others' mental states may be entirely different from one's own. In 2023, when GPT-3.5 was just born, psychologists were still surprised that AI possessed a Theory of Mind[14]. Today, within just a few short years, as large language models have become increasingly human-like, we are no longer surprised that they can understand users' emotions and intentions just as humans do.

　　Artificial intelligence has been widely applied in the field of psychological and emotional support. A meta-analysis points out that AI-based chatbot interventions have tremendous potential in alleviating depressive and anxiety symptoms in adults [15]. The report *Talk, Trust and Trade-Offs: How and Why Teens Use AI Companions* shows that 72% of American teens have used an AI companion at least once, and 52% use them regularly; 33% use them for social interactions/relationships (friendship, emotional support, romance, etc.); some teens find AI conversations more satisfying than those with real people. Furthermore, research indicates that AI companions are as effective as interacting with real people in alleviating loneliness, outperforming other activities like watching YouTube videos, with making the user feel heard being the key factor in relieving loneliness[16].

　　While generative AI replaces real people in providing emotional support and comfort, it can help those who have difficulty obtaining emotional support receive positive psychological feedback; however, it simultaneously diminishes the unique value of humans as emotional supporters.

　　Clearly, the prevalence of AI companions affects not only emotional and economic relationships between people but also has a profound impact on industries such as psychological counseling, entertainment, and social media, ultimately altering the overall economic and GDP structure.

　　Regarding the impact of AI companions on interpersonal emotional and economic relationships, let me give an example in the form of a fictional story:

　　*A lived in 2016 and wanted to find a girlfriend. So, he actively sought a partner on social networking/dating sites. He found a girlfriend and spent a considerable amount of money on her. Eventually, they got married and had children, and A spent a lot of money on getting married and raising offspring.*

　　*A lives in 2026 and wants to find a girlfriend. However, during the process of looking for a partner, he discovers that AI companions can provide more emotional value than real human partners. As a result, he loses interest in finding a real human girlfriend. He spends his money purchasing AI companion services and saves the rest, even feeling fortunate that he did not spend a massive amount of money on marriage and having children.*

　　Of course, people whose marriage and relationship trajectories undergo major changes due to AI influencing their mating strategies are only a minority, but this situation cannot be ignored.

　　If a person's emotional value decreases because their social circle is affected by AI, resulting in a reduction of human emotional support received (as their friends and family are more inclined to seek emotional support from AI), they are forced to seek emotional support from AI to compensate for this lack. This will create a vicious cycle.

　　A similar vicious cycle exists regarding AI's impact on employment: workers experience a devaluation of their labor due to the widespread use of AI tools. To increase the value of their labor, they actively use AI as well, making the use of AI tools even more pervasive.

　　Concerns about the devaluation of one's own value in the AI era can also trigger psychological problems such as anxiety and depression. In recent years, academia has proposed the concept of Artificial Intelligence Replacement Dysfunction (AIRD) to describe the psychological and existential distress experienced by individuals facing the threat or reality of job loss due to AI:
　
　*Clinically, AIRD is characterized by an intense and persistent fear of unemployment or personal obsolescence arising from artificial intelligence integration, which typically leads to significant functional impairment. Affected individuals may experience notable cognitive and emotional changes, such as delusions, feelings of worthlessness, resentment, and despair. These symptoms usually occur in the absence of other primary psychiatric disorders, substance abuse, or structural brain lesions[17].*
　
　A 2026 workplace report released by Modern Health, surveying 1,000 employees, showed that nearly 70% of employees (69%) believe AI will lead to layoffs at their companies within three years. Nearly half (49%) personally worry about losing their jobs to AI, which was cited as one of the most severe personal stressors in this survey. Furthermore, nearly two-thirds (63%) of employees reported that over the past year, they had used alcohol, THC/cannabis, or non-prescription drugs after work to relieve stress. More concerningly, 52% of employees even use these substances during the workday to cope with job stress. (Source: [U.S. Workforce in Mental Health Crisis Driven by AI Anxiety, Political Stress, and a Collapse in Employer Trust](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20260428972933/en/U.S.-Workforce-in-Mental-Health-Crisis-Driven-by-AI-Anxiety-Political-Stress-and-a-Collapse-in-Employer-Trust))

　　Additionally, studies show that using AI is associated with mental health issues. A survey of 20,847 US adults found that 10.3% of respondents reported using generative AI daily. A higher frequency of AI use is associated with a slight increase in depressive symptoms—those who use AI daily have a 30% higher probability of experiencing moderate to severe depression compared to infrequent users, a phenomenon particularly prominent among younger users[18]. Of course, this does not necessarily mean that AI increases the risk of depression, as people who use AI may inherently be more prone to depressive symptoms.

　　Although welfare policies like Unconditional Basic Income can compensate for livelihood issues caused by job loss, they cannot make up for the "sense of lost self-worth." The degree of the "sense of lost self-worth" is related to how much an individual values their own worth. If a person cherishes their self-worth, and the carrier of that cherished worth (personal abilities, physical body, etc.) happens to be easily replaceable by AI, then they are more likely to feel that their self-worth has been stripped away by AI. The widespread use of AI affects not only the evaluation of current self-worth but also the expectations for growth value. Let us imagine the following scenario:

　　*A highly gifted child in the pre-AI era envisions their life development as attending a good university, becoming a respected professional like a doctor or lawyer, working hard, and then having their own family and the desired middle-class life. They are full of hope for the future.*

　　*The same highly gifted child in the AI era foresees that by the time they reach adulthood, AGI will have been born. They cannot compete with AGI for the jobs they are interested in, realizing that no matter how hard they study now, they will never attain the life they desire in the future. AI destroys their expectations for the future and even brings them despair.*

　　Human value sometimes stems from disparities. For instance, when we say a doctoral student has a higher value than a primary school student, it is because there is an educational gap between them. People sometimes evaluate their "self-worth" through comparison with others. Today, AI is reducing the disparities between people in certain aspects. For example: a person who does not know how to paint can use prompts to have a painting model generate an image in a short time. A professional artist using the same model will generate a better image due to their aesthetics and skills, but this gap will be smaller than if both produced images by hand-drawing. In this situation, the professional artist's evaluation of their "self-worth," generated through comparison with others, will diminish. The gap between people is reflected not only in talent but also in whether that talent can be translated into value (value to oneself or to others). Suppose a person is proficient in five languages, but the advantage this language talent gives them in boosting their self-worth evaluation regarding employment, mate selection, and socializing weakens; then, the value of their language talent diminishes. The prevalence of large language models has clearly reduced the value of language talent. As AI becomes increasingly intelligent, richer in form, and capable of taking on more and more human work, the value created by interpersonal disparities will become smaller and smaller. Perhaps in the future, AI can help humanity achieve unprecedented civilizational accomplishments, such as eliminating poverty, conquering aging, achieving immortality, and interstellar travel. If the price of human immortality is the contraction or even loss of human self-worth, is this the future we want?

　　Even more terrifyingly, superintelligence might lead humanity into an era of "Behavioral Sink." Ethologist John B. Calhoun conducted a series of experiments, including "Universe 25," in the 1960s and 1970s. To study the effects of overpopulation on social behavior, Calhoun simulated a "mouse utopia": an infinite supply of food and water, regular cleaning, constant temperature, and an absence of natural predators. The experiment began with 8 mice (4 males and 4 females). The population grew rapidly over the course of the experiment—which lasted over a thousand days—reaching a peak of 2,200 on day 560, before precipitously declining toward extinction. During this process, extreme social pathologies emerged: male mice refused to court and attacked their peers, while female mice even abandoned their pups. Despite abundant resources, the mice lost their normal instinctive behaviors. A group of individuals known as the "Beautiful Ones" appeared; they only ate and slept, ceasing to reproduce or engage in complex social activities, which ultimately led the "mouse utopia" to population extinction. Recent studies suggest that the collapse of the mouse utopia was caused by "Hierarchy Entropy Degeneration"—initially, high uncertainty drove the mice to participate in competition, reproduction, and parenting; however, when the social hierarchy became completely transparent and fixed, the expected return of participating in social activities dropped to zero, causing the mice to turn to "withdrawal" and "non-participation" behaviors[19]. Therefore, judging from these research findings, the demise of the mouse utopia was due to the mice losing hope in social activities and no longer finding them meaningful. From a neuroscience perspective, the brains of the mice no longer perceived social activities as having incentive salience.

　　Mouse Utopia and the era of future superintelligence seem to share some similarities. Superintelligence could eliminate poverty and satisfy our infinite material demands. Superintelligence will level the differences between people and obliterate the value of human growth (prior to which, social classes will have already become severely rigidly stratified). Superintelligence will become our best companion (and we will no longer seek human companions). When we see that our future is simply "lying flat" to be provided for by superintelligence, what else can we do but lie flat? Lying flat is still a relatively peaceful lifestyle. When people cannot find meaning in life, they might seek amusement through extreme means (such as resorting to violence, just like the male mice attacking their own kind in the Mouse Utopia experiment), or commit suicide out of despair.

　　To prevent the mouse utopia from becoming our future, we need a sanctuary where humanity can realize its self-worth. We can envision a future form of human community like this:

　　In the era of superintelligence, a human autonomous zone (perhaps initially a network state) emerges that limits the use of AI. In this community, the level of AI use is restricted so as not to deprive humans of their self-esteem and value. Community members gain a sense of worth through labor. In this community, professions that have largely been replaced by AI in the superintelligence era, such as lawyers, writers, and painters, are still entirely held by humans. For the possibility of such communities existing, we can look to the Amish. The Amish are a branch of the Protestant Anabaptist Christian movement, primarily residing in the United States and Canada. Due to their religious beliefs, the Amish actively choose to live an agrarian lifestyle akin to the Middle Ages or early 19th century, rejecting most modern technology.


**The Impact of Anti-Aging Technology on Reward and Emotional Systems**

*I wish to buy osmanthus and load up on wine, yet it's never the same as a journey in youth.*
*— Liu Guo (Song Dynasty), "Tang Duo Ling: Reed Leaves Fill the Islet"*

　　Adult readers have likely realized that as we grow older, it becomes increasingly difficult to feel joy. The reasons for this are manifold: on one hand, our dopamine systems are bombarded with various stimuli, causing our reward thresholds to rise; on the other hand, aging is accompanied by changes in monoamine levels and increased monoamine oxidase (MAO) activity[20]. 

  Furthermore, as we age, the volume and weight of our brains decrease; research shows that after age 40, brain volume and weight decline at a rate of approximately 5% per decade[21].

　　Theoretically, by reversing brain aging and improving the dopamine system (such as by increasing dopamine receptors and reducing the accumulation of ΔFosB), we can reduce stimulus tolerance and restore the sensitivity of the brain’s reward system to natural rewards or low-intensity stimuli.

　　Below are potential cell biology mechanisms and pharmacological methods for increasing dopamine receptors, enhancing receptor sensitivity, reducing ΔFosB accumulation, and mitigating addiction symptoms:

　　**AMPK Signaling Pathway:** The AMPK pathway is a key sensor of cellular energy status. It is activated during energy deficits to inhibit anabolism and promote catabolism to maintain energy homeostasis. In addiction and dopamine-related pathologies, AMPK activation can promote the degradation of ΔFosB through the autophagy pathway, thereby reducing its abnormal accumulation[22]. AMPK slows the desensitization of D2 autoreceptors by enhancing the action of D1-like receptors, strengthening energy homeostasis and indirectly supporting the recovery of dopamine receptor sensitivity[23].

　　**mTOR Signaling Pathway:** The mTOR pathway is a central regulatory network integrating signals from nutrients, energy, and growth factors, primarily functioning by promoting protein synthesis and cell growth. The mTOR inhibitor Rapamycin is one of the most widely recognized anti-aging drugs. The mTOR pathway has been confirmed as a regulator of neuroplasticity in the central nervous system. Inhibiting mTOR can reduce or block the abnormal accumulation of ΔFosB, especially under pathological conditions. Acute cocaine exposure activates the mTOR pathway in the prefrontal cortex, ventral tegmental area (VTA), and nucleus accumbens (NAc); systemic administration of Rapamycin to inhibit mTOR can block cocaine-induced locomotor sensitization[24]. 5-HTP alleviates levodopa-induced dyskinesia (LID) by modulating overactivated striatal AKT/mTOR/S6K and CREB/ΔFosB signaling pathways[25].

　　**MAPK Signaling Pathway:** The MAPK pathway transduces external stimuli to the cell nucleus via phosphorylation cascades, regulating gene expression, cell proliferation, and stress responses. As a key downstream pathway of the dopamine D1 receptor, MAPK/ERK activation follows the "D1R-cAMP/PKA-Ras/Raf-MEK-ERK" cascade axis. Research shows that acute drug exposure activates ERK, leading to the transcription of immediate early genes (including FosB); under chronic exposure, persistent ERK activation promotes ΔFosB accumulation (via transcriptional induction and stabilization), driving addiction-related plasticity such as increased dendritic spines and altered reward sensitivity[23]. Luteolin can inhibit methamphetamine-induced behavioral sensitization by blocking the ERK/ΔFosB pathway[26]. The MEK inhibitor Trametinib has gained attention in recent years as an anti-aging drug; combined inhibition of mTOR and MAPK pathways (Rapamycin plus Trametinib) produces a significant synergistic life-extension effect in mice, increasing median lifespan by approximately 27% to 29%, outperforming monotherapy[27].

　　It is worth noting that Luteolin has broad targets, including activating AMPK and inhibiting GSK-3 and CK2. Studies show that a standardized extract of Salvia haenkei* extended the median lifespan of naturally aging mice from 28 months to 32.25 months [28], with Luteolin being the most abundant flavonoid in the extract.

　　**NF-κB Signaling Pathway:** NF-κB (Nuclear Factor-κB) is a family of transcription factors (including p65/RelA, p50, c-Rel, etc.) that regulate inflammation, immunity, cell survival, and plasticity by activating downstream target genes (such as cytokines, BDNF, and structural proteins). Chronic cocaine administration upregulates NF-κB signaling in the nucleus accumbens (NAc), modulating neuronal morphology and cocaine reward. Inhibiting NF-κB in the NAc can reduce cocaine-induced conditioned place preference (CPP), self-administration, and cue-induced seeking behavior, with sex differences observed (more pronounced in males) [29].

　　**cAMP/PKA Signaling Pathway:** This pathway uses cAMP as a second messenger to regulate various cellular functions via Protein Kinase A (PKA) and participates in transcriptional regulation. The dopamine D2 receptor (D2R) belongs to the D2-like receptor family, coupled with Gi/Go proteins, and reduces cAMP by inhibiting adenylate cyclase. Forskolin activates cAMP, causing negative feedback or compensatory adaptation; cells then increase D2 receptor expression through cAMP/PKA-dependent transcriptional mechanisms to become more sensitive to dopamine. Cell model studies show that treating Ltk- cells with 100 μM forskolin for 16 hours upregulates D2long (D2L) receptor numbers by 43–96%[30]. In vivo models show that continuous intracerebroventricular (i.c.v.) infusion of forskolin in rats for 5 days increases striatal D2 receptor density in a concentration-dependent manner[31]. (Notably, Forskolin is a core component of chemical reprogramming.)

　　**Epigenetics:** Epigenetics significantly regulates dopamine receptors (especially DRD2, as well as DRD1). Mechanisms such as DNA methylation, histone modification, and non-coding RNA influence receptor gene expression, density, and downstream signaling, participating in the development of schizophrenia, addiction, stress/depression, and Parkinson’s disease[32]. Histone deacetylase inhibitors (HDACi) promote gene transcription (such as BDNF and IEGs) and enhance synaptic plasticity by increasing histone acetylation (especially H3/H4). Studies show that SAHA (Vorinostat, a broad-spectrum HDACi) can partially rescue depression-like behavior in Crtc1⁻/⁻ mice, accompanied by increased BDNF expression in the prefrontal cortex[33]. Valproic acid, a common HDACi used clinically for epilepsy and bipolar disorder, is also a core component of chemical reprogramming; mouse studies show it can reduce ethanol intake, CPP, and self-administration behavior[34].

　A popular view is that long-lived elderly people have optimistic mindsets. Is there a scientific basis for the relationship between mindset and lifespan? In the Big Five Inventory, Neuroticism is the core dimension measuring emotional stability. It describes the frequency and intensity with which an individual triggers negative emotions and their response to stress. People high in neuroticism have high emotional volatility, while those low in neuroticism are peaceful. Research using the NEO-FFI scale found that offspring of centenarians (regardless of gender) scored significantly lower in Neuroticism and higher in Extraversion than the norm. Researchers believe low neuroticism helps better regulate stress and maintain healthy behaviors, supporting "successful aging" and potential longevity[35].

　　Chronic inflammation is one of the Hallmarks of Aging[36]. There is a bidirectional cycle between chronic inflammation and the development of age-related diseases like cardiovascular disease, neurodegeneration, cancer, and frailty[37]. A key trait of centenarians is low inflammation; in the extremely elderly, chronic inflammation (rather than telomere length) is the most important predictor of mortality risk, cognitive function, and independence. Centenarians and their offspring have significantly lower inflammation levels[38]. They also possess unique immune systems; centenarians mitigate "inflammaging" by reducing the Th17/Treg cell ratio and shifting them toward an anti-inflammatory secretory phenotype, significantly lowering pro-inflammatory cytokines like IL-17A, IL-1β, and IL-23[39].

　　The relationship between anxiety/depression and inflammation is a vital area of psychoneuroimmunology. Meta-analyses and cohort studies consistently show that patients with anxiety disorders have higher levels of peripheral pro-inflammatory markers than healthy controls. A 2019 systematic review (16 studies) found significantly elevated C-reactive protein (CRP) in patients with Generalized Anxiety Disorder[40]. A 2018 meta-analysis of anxiety, PTSD, and OCD showed an overall increase in pro-inflammatory factors[41]. A 2025 Lifelines cohort study of ~55,098 people found that CRP Genetic Risk Scores (CRP GRS) are associated with an increased risk of anxiety disorders[42]. Ample evidence shows a strong, bidirectional link between depression and inflammation: inflammation can induce or maintain depression, while chronic stress promotes inflammation. A meta-analysis of 5,166 patients found significantly elevated blood levels of CRP, IL-6, IL-12, and TNF-α in depressed individuals[43].

　　In summary, the optimism of centenarians may be linked to lower inflammation levels. If we aren't born with the low neuroticism and low inflammation of a centenarian, can we compensate for it? We can intervene in signaling pathways to reduce inflammation (e.g., NF-κB, JAK-STAT, MAPK, cGAS-STING, PI3K/Akt/mTOR). Several anti-aging drugs/supplements show anti-inflammatory potential. Luteolin inhibits NF-κB to reduce pro-inflammatory cytokines [44]. The Rapamycin and Trametinib combination significantly reduces age-related inflammation in the brain, kidneys, and spleen of mice[27].

　　*(Interestingly, in recent years, after my own aggressive anti-aging interventions, my Big Five Extraversion increased, Neuroticism decreased, and Agreeableness increased, mirroring the patterns found in centenarian research.)*

　　As mentioned earlier, surveys show that some employees turn to drugs or alcohol when facing workplace stress. My suggestion is that rather than using unhealthy or addictive substances for self-numbing, it is better to use anti-aging drugs or supplements to combat negative emotions. This is far more beneficial for physical and mental health. For those worried about their jobs being replaced by AI next year, living to 120 might not be their immediate concern, but the stress and negative emotions affecting their work today are impossible to ignore. However, using anti-aging supplements to improve dopamine tolerance is still rare; our medical system is not yet prepared for this, and the concept is not widely known. While "natural" methods (diet, sleep, exercise) can help, they are often luxuries for busy workers. When our genes cannot adapt to the times and natural methods aren't enough to fight "evolutionary mismatch," using biotechnology to regulate the body and adapt to the modern era is a choice worth considering.
　　Against this backdrop, we can imagine a cyber-tragedy:

　　*An AI recommends an ideal human partner to a clever AI user, based on historical human romance patterns. Because the user has been taking drugs to reduce ΔFosB accumulation, they still retain an interest in real human relationships. They plan meticulously and finally find the person the AI recommended.However, the reward threshold of the recommended person has been significantly raised by digital entertainment and blockchain technology, and they are heavily reliant on generative AI for information and companionship. They have already lost interest in real interpersonal relationships. Meanwhile, the first person, having developed expectations for this "ideal partner" based on a perfect predictive model, finds it difficult to ever feel anticipation for anyone else. In this story, it is a case of "Success by AI, Failure by AI."*


**Our Future**

*We should regard it as a privilege to be stepping stones to higher things.*
*— Arthur C. Clarke*

　　There is significant divergence within the industry regarding whether AI will replace human jobs on a large scale. Geoffrey Hinton, the "Godfather of Deep Learning," belongs to the pessimistic camp, believing that AI poses a long-term/existential risk (where AI surpasses humanity). He estimates the probability of AI causing human extinction within the next 30 years to be 10%–20%[45]. Yann LeCun holds a more optimistic view, arguing that history has proven technological changes ultimately create jobs rather than destroy them[46]. LeCun admires the research of economists such as Philippe Aghion, Erik Brynjolfsson, Daron Acemoglu, Andrew McAfee, and David Autor [43], and he believes that AI researchers and CEOs of AI companies—such as Hinton and Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei—lack expertise on how technological revolutions shape the labor market[47].

　　While institutions can certainly buffer the impact of AI on the job market and mitigate other threats, can they keep pace with the speed of AI development? The intelligence level of AI has advanced incredibly fast in recent years. According to the Stanford AI Index 2025, the difficulty of challenging benchmarks introduced just in 2023 was rapidly diminished by 2024: MMMU improved by 18.8 percentage points, GPQA improved by 48.9 percentage points, and the SWE-bench resolution rate jumped from 4.4% to 71.7%[48].

　　As AI becomes increasingly powerful, its psychological impact on humans will also intensify. The APA's *2025 Stress in America* report states that 65% of adults aged 18–34 report AI-related stress, up from 52% the previous year, indicating a significant rise in AI-induced stress[49]. Is our mental health system prepared for these ever-growing AI-related psychological issues? According to the *State of the Behavioral Health Workforce, 2025* report, approximately 62 million US adults (23% of the adult population) suffered from a mental illness in 2024. Nearly half of them (48%) received no treatment, with an average wait time of 48 days. Even with insurance, many abandoned treatment due to high costs, an inability to find in-network providers, or insufficient coverage[50]. In China, the scarcity of mental health resources is even more severe than in the US. The World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that in China, approximately 54 million people suffer from depression and 41 million from anxiety disorders (out of a total population of roughly 1.4 billion) [51]. By the end of 2021, the ratio of psychiatrists in China was approximately 3.6 per 100,000 population (accounting for 1.49% of all physicians nationwide) [52]. In contrast, according to the *WHO Mental Health Atlas 2014*, the average ratio of psychiatrists in developed countries was 6.6 per 100,000[53]. This webpage displays a visualization of psychiatrist density by country based on the *WHO Mental Health Atlas 2020*: https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/psychiatrists-working-in-the-mental-health-sector

　　In *My techno-optimism*, Ethereum founder Vitalik Buterin proposed the technological philosophy framework "d/acc" (decentralized and democratic differential defensive acceleration). A key tenet of this framework is the belief that we should prioritize accelerating technologies that enhance our defensive capabilities. Under the d/acc framework, anti-aging technology is not only a vital tool for extending lifespan, treating age-related diseases, and reducing the suffering caused by them, but it is also a crucial technology for defending against the potential harm AI may inflict on human psychology.

　　Perhaps we will ultimately be unable to prevent a silicon-based civilization from replacing our carbon-based one. However, through biotechnology, we can provide some buffering room for the human mind during a process that will inevitably bring immense pain to humanity—allowing us to still retain a bit of curiosity about all things, a bit of appreciation for human value, a bit of expectation for human connections, and to keep our hearts full of hope.

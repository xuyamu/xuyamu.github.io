　　Some people believe that the film *Nymphomaniac* is about feminism, and there are two diametrically opposed opinions regarding this:

　　One perspective argues that the film supports and sympathizes with feminism. This interpretation suggests that by creating a rebellious female character like Joe, who pursues sexual liberation, the film shows support, concern, and sympathy for feminism.

　　Another perspective argues that the film is anti-feminist. This interpretation believes that the film's portrayal of Joe is overly negative, and that this negativity is built upon an anti-feminist stance, while the shooting of the feminist Seligman is seen as a symbol of anti-feminism.

　　What is feminism? Here, I will borrow a definition from Wikipedia: Feminism (the feminist movement) refers to social theories and political movements primarily motivated by and originating from female experiences. Besides critiquing social relations, many proponents of feminism also focus on analyzing gender inequality and promoting women's rights, interests, and issues.

　　According to this definition, if we do not view the film from a so-called feminist perspective, it can simply be described as a story about addiction. No matter what a person is overly addicted to (drugs, sex, animal hoarding, etc.), it becomes very difficult for them to keep their life in order. Throughout the narrative of the entire film, Joe does not suffer much discrimination or oppression from men, nor does she encounter unequal treatment based on her gender. Instead, she frequently appears before the audience in the posture of a strong person and a conqueror. The misfortunes she encounters are mostly caused by her own deviation from the norm (if a man were addicted to sex and neglected his duties, his outcome would generally not be much better either). Therefore, it can be said that this film does not heavily involve the many issues of feminism. The reason why there are two diametrically opposed interpretations regarding its feminist themes is precisely because the film originally does not touch upon feminism much, leaving interpreters to force connections and over-interpret it.

　　Therefore, I do not think the film *Nymphomaniac* either supports or opposes feminism. I believe that, at most, it only satirizes certain vulgarized feminists. I define "vulgarized feminists" as a group of people who indiscriminately attribute any unfortunate treatment suffered by women (especially when these misfortunes are related to sex) to the oppression of the patriarchal system, all while feeling a sense of moral superiority. The character in the film—Joe's listener, Seligman—is exactly such a dogmatic and vulgarized feminist. He attempts to use popular feminist ideologies to explain the misfortunes of the female protagonist, Joe, but is met with her sarcasm. Joe comments on Seligman: “Although all this sounds frighteningly close to cliches of our times.”

　　Labeling oneself a feminist is a very simple thing, much easier than labeling oneself a scientist or a philosopher. This is because feminism is not an honorary title bestowed by society; it has no threshold. Anyone can proclaim themselves a feminist without facing excessive criticism or mockery. As a result, society is filled with various vulgarized feminists. Some of them have paranoid tendencies, are overly sensitive, and are prone to jumping at shadows, frequently throwing labels at others such as "anti-feminist," "feudal remnant," or "defender of patriarchy." To a large extent, these individuals have faced criticism regarding gender and sexuality (such as for pursuing sexual liberation), so they borrow feminism to defend themselves or counter-attack dissidents. They do not care about academic issues, nor do they care about the substance and history of gender dynamics; they merely want to use feminism to embolden themselves and boast of their superiority. (Of course, Seligman does not belong to this specific group; he just likes to drop literary references, or in other words, act pretentious.)

　　Many people are dissatisfied with the ending for the character Seligman, where he is ultimately shot by Joe after an unsuccessful attempt to have sex with her. This dissatisfaction stems from a suspicion: after living a sexless life for so many years, Seligman suddenly wants to have sex with the female protagonist. Isn't this transition too abrupt?

　　People who live a sexless life are generally referred to as asexuals or ascetics. So which one is Seligman? The film doesn't spend much ink on this. Therefore, Seligman's behavior is truly unfathomable.

　　The so-called asexuality, according to the usual definition, refers to a psychological tendency characterized by a lack of interest in sexual behavior (this definition is relatively broad). Wikipedia defines asexuality as such: "Asexuality (or nonsexuality) is the lack of sexual attraction to anyone or low or absent interest in sexual activity." The formation of asexuality involves complex psychological factors; asexuals make up only 1% of the population, and asexual men are even rarer.

　　Asceticism, on the other hand, refers to a theoretical system or attitude toward life that involves restraining one's desires (especially sexual desire) to achieve a certain purpose. Many resources on asexuality strictly demarcate the conceptual boundaries between asceticism and asexuality. This perspective argues that asceticism is a result imposed by external conditions, while asexuality is an innate, instinctual reaction of the asexual individual. I believe this black-and-white categorization is unreliable. "Coming from within" or "an instinctual reaction" is a vague state. As asexuality is a psychological tendency, it is difficult to determine exactly to what extent it is influenced by genetic factors versus acquired environments and ideologies. If we define asceticism as the principles upheld by ascetics, then it is also a question whether ascetics still have an interest in engaging in sexual activities after a long period of abstinence (if they lose interest, according to the usual definition, they have already become asexual). In addition, generally speaking, so-called ascetics strive to eliminate all sexual behaviors, including masturbation and sexual relations with others, while often also avoiding romantic emotional attachments with others. Asexuals, however, are merely unwilling to have sexual intercourse with others, but they can still harbor romantic feelings for people.

　　Perhaps we can define it like this: for ascetics, there is a strong internal ideological conflict over whether or not to engage in sexual behavior with others, but they ultimately choose abstinence. For asexuals, although they might reject sexual behavior due to certain experiences or the influence of certain ideologies, their choice to reject sex does not require intense internal conflict. However, how to measure this so-called "intense internal conflict" remains a debatable question.

　　Furthermore, asceticism is a label that appeared much earlier than asexuality (the term "asexual" can only be traced back to 1830, whereas in ancient Greece, there was already a word for asceticism—ἄσκησις). Either way, both are a sort of assertion of identity, or rather, a label. In an era without the concept of "asexuality," many asexuals might have used "asceticism" to label themselves. For instance, we can imagine that many religious saints who practiced asceticism were themselves asexuals; compared to allosexuals, it was easier for them to accept a celibate life.

　　It is possible that the director, Lars von Trier, simply doesn't understand asexuality or asceticism at all. Therefore, in the end, he could only rely on his own assumptions about asexuality or asceticism to create a character like Seligman, going so far as to give him such a baffling and controversial ending.

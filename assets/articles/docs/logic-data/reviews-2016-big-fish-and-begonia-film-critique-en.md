　　I had heard about *Big Fish & Begonia* many years ago, but I didn't follow the film closely over the long term. Therefore, while I had some expectations for the movie, I wouldn't consider myself a nostalgic die-hard fan. What truly caught my attention was the massive amount of reviews and shares on my WeChat Moments and from the official accounts I follow after the film's release (I know a bunch of filmmakers and animators, and due to my major and background, I can be considered half an industry insider). Before watching the film, I read some reviews on Douban and knew there were fiercely negative critiques. Some of these scathing reviews finalized their verdict on the film using terms like "twisted values," "Mary Sue," and "melodramatic." I have always scoffed at or disapproved of such comments. This is because I believe that people who think a good film must have "politically correct values" lack not only imagination and independent thinking skills, but also the ability and courage to be mavericks. Furthermore, using "Mary Sue" to describe a character is often an exaggerated and emotional reaction (the likelihood of a true Mary Sue existing in a widely promoted, decently produced theatrical release like *Big Fish & Begonia* is extremely low). As for those who use "melodramatic" to judge others' emotional relationships, it is usually because they can neither resonate with those relationships nor understand them through logic, common sense, or theory. Because the emotional intensity is too overwhelming for them to accept, they resort to the blanket term "melodramatic" to express their vague impressions.

　　First, let me address my doubts regarding some common viewpoints:

　　Viewpoint: Chun harmed her people for the sake of love.
　　Rebuttal: Chun's initial motive for saving Kun was to repay a debt of gratitude and to rid herself of the guilt that her savior died for her. She was completely unaware of the natural disasters that would subsequently occur. She only knew she was trading half her lifespan for Kun's resurrection. If it weren't for the subsequent disasters, saving Kun would have just been a private matter. The fact that things escalated and involved her people was not something Chun could have foreseen. The blame can only be placed on Chun being "too young, too simple" and on the Soul Keeper (Lingpo) for not explaining the potential consequences clearly. (The Soul Keeper is quite scheming and perhaps harbored a conspiracy—his remark after the flood, "It's time to wash things clean down below," suggests he might have intentionally guided the disaster.) If the Soul Keeper had told Chun about the consequences of divine wrath, would she still have resurrected Kun without hesitation? If so, she probably would have faced a grueling dilemma. Using one's own life to save another is inherently a noble act; there was nothing immoral about Chun's original intention. Unfortunately, her good intentions led to a bad outcome. Therefore, the claim that "Chun harmed her people for love" is a one-sided view that ignores plot logic and character motivation.

　　Viewpoint: The characters' dialogues are baffling, childish, and "chuunibyou" (adolescent delusions).
　　Qiu's mention of "the love of a god" and Chun's line "you treat me as well as a brother" are two of the most criticized lines. However, looking at the overall plot and the characters' state of mind, they might not be as unreasonable as some viewers think. Qiu's line about "the love of a god" comes after he has been drinking. It doesn't seem strange that, fueled by alcohol and emotional agitation, he would say something highly passionate and irrational. This line might also hint at Qiu's connection to the gods (perhaps he is a descendant of a god? His ability to open the sky suggests this). As for Chun, an innocent teenage girl experiencing her first awakening of love in a simple utopia, it is not entirely unreasonable for her to respond to Qiu's confession with seemingly low emotional intelligence. After all, she has no dating experience, and her life lacks dating guides, romance novels, or romantic movies to provide her with direction. If her response were overly mature or formulaic, it would contradict her identity. Saying "you treat me as well as a brother" doesn't mean she is ungrateful or handing out a "friend-zone card." Can't a little sister fall in love with the boy next door? Qiu only indirectly hinted that he thought Chun was beautiful. If Chun had instantly caught his drift, it would have made her seem too experienced, conflicting with the personality and mindset she developed in her specific environment. The fact that Chun wanted to go home with Qiu after sending Kun away, and wept bitterly when Qiu sacrificed himself, proves that she cared deeply for him. Therefore, I believe Chun's "brother" line was not a heartless rejection, but a heartfelt expression from an innocent, inexperienced young girl.

　　Of course, these blunt and low-EQ lines inevitably lower the impression adult audiences have of the film. Why do some viewers feel cringe when hearing such lines? I suspect a major reason is that, in adulthood, some people cannot face childish behavior. Childish actions remind them of their own past immaturity, and recalling this immaturity makes them cringe.

　　Cringe is a highly emotional reaction often tied to personal experience. Therefore, for a film to avoid triggering secondhand embarrassment in its audience, it needs to maintain a detached aesthetic distance in its atmosphere. I will discuss this in more detail below.

　　Naturally, the screenwriters could have written the characters to be more mature and avoided cringe-inducing dialogues, but that would require changing the character settings. For example, they could have made Chun and Qiu calm, precocious, and highly intelligent youths. But in that case, many plot points would become illogical, because much of the story in *Big Fish & Begonia* is built upon the protagonists' innocence, ignorance, and reckless audacity. If Chun were calm and precocious, she would have thoroughly investigated the details of divine wrath upon first meeting the Soul Keeper, rather than unquestioningly trading half her life for Kun's. Without that, the subsequent plot could not unfold.

　　Next, I'd like to discuss the areas where *Big Fish & Begonia* has room for improvement. I bring these up in the hope that the film can do better. There are many mindless, unconstructive haters online, but they only vent their emotions without pointing the film in the right direction.

　　A widely criticized aspect of *Big Fish & Begonia* is its focus on the emotional entanglements of three innocent youths as its primary subject matter. This makes the film's scope feel somewhat small. Perhaps such a theme is easy to digest for specific demographics (like inexperienced teenagers), but for adult audiences, this pacing and focus might be harder to accept. After all, *Big Fish & Begonia* is not a children's movie, so it is necessary to give the film a grander scope.

　　How do you enlarge a film's scope? A film's scope is reflected in its worldview, its philosophy of life, and its ideological perspective.

　　Let's start with the worldview. *Big Fish & Begonia* has a highly imaginative worldview. The world isn't massive, but it is extremely unique, which is a strength. However, the film fails to flesh out this unique world. Chun and her people live a utopian lifestyle, much like residents of an ordinary rural village. Yet, these people are demigod-like beings with superpowers. With great power comes great responsibility. If capable individuals harbor ambitions, power struggles ensue; if they decide to do something monumental, the world turns upside down. So, what complex relationships exist among Chun's people? Can they really maintain such simplistic neighborly relations? What do they actually do with their powers? In Greek mythology, the relationships among the gods are intricate and full of stories. Granted, there are childish elements, but the tales are rich and colorful. By comparison, while the world of *Big Fish & Begonia* is unique, it feels a bit thin. When compared to mature fantasy works with massive worldviews like *The Lord of the Rings* or *A Song of Ice and Fire*, it seems decidedly "too young, too simple."

　　Next, let's talk about the philosophy of life. As I analyzed earlier, this film absolutely does not promote a narrow view of love where one abandons family and destroys the world in the name of romance, as some imagine. But we must admit that the protagonists are naive and willful. What values does this film actually present to us? I summarize them as follows:

　　1. The spirit of self-sacrifice to save others. Kun sacrifices his life to save Chun, Chun trades half her lifespan to save Kun, and Qiu sacrifices himself to save Chun. These all represent self-sacrifice.
　　2. Rebelling against authority in the name of love. Qiu opposes the gods and opens the sky to help Chun and Kun, which is an act of defying authority.
　　3. Acting recklessly and disregarding consequences to save someone. Chun ignores the consequences to save a life, even triggering divine wrath that affects her people. This is reckless.

　　In the eyes of many, the third value is a sign of a twisted moral compass, because acting recklessly is a sign of immaturity. Furthermore, many viewers feel the film is centered around a love triangle (likely because the dynamic between the three leads leaves the deepest impression), which also makes the film's scope feel small.

　　So how could a grander scope be achieved in its philosophy of life? If the protagonists' innocence and willfulness are required by the plot, the writers could easily have endowed them with more rebellious thoughts. In the film, Qiu and Chun discuss the possibility of the existence of gods, but the exploration is not deep. The film could have been much more profound in its portrayal of Qiu's curiosity about the gods and his rebellion against them. If Qiu possessed more curiosity and critical thinking skills, he should have asked:

　　What kind of existence are he and his people?
　　Why are he and his people born with magic and the ability to control natural phenomena?
　　Why does resurrecting someone invoke divine wrath (and why does it implicate not just the initiator, but their entire clan)?
　　Where are the gods? What gives the gods the right to determine life and death and unleash divine wrath?
　　How is divine wrath generated?

　　If Qiu pondered these questions, his character wouldn't just be a passionate youth acting willfully and sacrificing his life for love; he would be a thinker. Although Chun, Qiu, and their people possess immense magical power, they all lack intellectual depth; their lifestyle and cognitive levels remain in a rather primitive state. Notably, Chun's people actually refer to the big fish—Kun's soul—as a "monster." Demonizing the unknown is an incredibly ignorant behavior. Having a thinker emerge within intellectually primitive community to struggle against backward mindsets is highly dramatic. If the film had expanded its plot in this direction, its scope would have felt much grander.

　　Finally, let's discuss the ideological perspective. I mentioned earlier that to avoid triggering secondhand embarrassment (cringe), the film needs to maintain a somewhat detached distance from the audience's subjective emotions. An important way to achieve this is by giving the film greater intellectual depth. Profound thoughts rarely cause cringe because they are usually distanced from personal subjective experiences and daily life. As analyzed above, cringe is an experience closely related to personal emotions. Therefore, deep philosophy is less likely to trigger it. Watching a K-drama might make you cringe, but reading a history of philosophy won't, because the latter is far removed from the individual (especially ordinary people) and doesn't easily trigger emotional reactions based on personal experience. Moreover, profound thoughts can sometimes evoke a sense of the sublime, and the sublime can sometimes neutralize cringe (for example, getting soaked while jumping into water to save someone is embarrassing, but the sublimity of the life-saving act makes people overlook the embarrassment of being soaked).

　　As previously analyzed, childish actions and words easily make audiences cringe. *Big Fish & Begonia* contains cringe-inducing elements not only in its dialogue but also in its voiceover narration. The film's narration occasionally features sentences that feel incredibly casual. For example, one line goes: "There are some things you just have a feeling about..." Casual narration makes people cringe (viewers might think: "Why does this sound like the essays I wrote in middle school just to appease my teacher?"), and it also reduces the film's intellectual rigor.

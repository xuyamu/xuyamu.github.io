　　 The broadcast of *The Lost Tomb* seasonal drama has triggered a massive wave of savage roasting. Everyone is calling it a terrible show, but where exactly did this highly anticipated production go wrong? I would like to offer some personal insights on the matter.

　　 Below, I will conduct a comprehensive critique of this TV series from various aspects of film production. Please note: the scope of this review covers *only* the publicly released Pilot Episode Part 1 and Part 2.

**I. Scriptwriting**

　　 The fact that *The Lost Tomb* (literally "Grave Robbers’ Chronicles") was turned into "Archaeologists’ Chronicles" has already been mocked to death by countless people, but that isn't even the plot's most fatal flaw (even an "Archaeology Chronicles" could have been made entertaining). The reason this show (or at least its pilot) is so bad isn't that it deviates too much from the original novel—adaptation is not inherently a bad thing—but making an absolute mess of an adaptation can only result in garbage.

**1. Characterization**

　　 What got roasted the most in this series are its two original characters: High Shao (a name that screams cringe subculture—my very first thought upon seeing it was a drug addict!) and Chen Chengcheng (which reminds me of a contemporary artist I know named "Chen Chen Chen", also jokingly nicknamed "Chen Cubed"—though that person is infinitely more brilliant than this character). The problem with these two is that they are portrayed in an overly exaggerated, pretentious manner that completely contradicts their established backgrounds. According to the show’s lore, High Shao is an overseas student specializing in high tech. Logically, someone skilled in high tech should be a character of exceptionally high IQ. Yet on screen, High Shao displays none of the sharp wit he is supposed to possess, coming across instead as a flashy, useless dandy. By comparison, high-tech characters like the scientists in *The Big Bang Theory* or the computer genius Harold Finch in *Person of Interest* are infinitely more fleshed out and three-dimensional. As for Chen Chengcheng, an archaeology major, her behavior and mindset similarly fail to match her background. For instance, when Wu Sansheng asks her what skills she brings to the table, she is utterly clueless. I looked up the standard curriculum for archaeology majors and found that it typically includes the following courses:

　　 Introduction to Archaeology, Paleolithic Archaeology, Neolithic Archaeology, Xia-Shang-Zhou Archaeology, Warring States-Qin-Han Archaeology, Wei-Jin-Sui-Tang Archaeology, Song-Yuan-Ming Archaeology, and so on.

　　 Given that archaeology students must undergo such rigorous academic training, I genuinely want to ask the hopelessly amateurish Chen Chengcheng: *"How on earth did you get admitted to the archaeology department, and what did you even do in class?"* Seriously, screenwriters—are you sure you weren’t just trying to smear archaeology majors? Not only is Chen Chengcheng professionally inept, but her demeanor is also childish, theatrical, and fundamentally fake. A persona like this feels completely out of place in a suspense-adventure series like *The Lost Tomb*.

　　 Why are the characters portrayed as so exaggerated, pretentious, and unrealistic? The root cause lies in the screenwriters' lack of basic craft and sincerity. To be clear: when I say "screenwriters" here, I am not pointing fingers solely at the names listed in the credits, but rather at a collective entity. During production, credited writers don’t always get the final say on the script; in fact, ever since the show aired, the two credited screenwriters have been crying foul over having to take the fall for a final script they had no control over. The world is complex. To reasonably capture an immensely complex world within the confines of a limited work of fiction requires sharp observation, profound insight, and strong narrative control. A person's behavior and speech patterns are rooted in complex psychological mechanisms; even the most brainless remark, when examined closely, can be explained through intricate psychology. If a screenwriter fails to think deeply about a character's psyche—settling instead for depicting superficial traits or outright copying third-rate tropes—the characters will inevitably turn out exaggerated, pretentious, one-dimensional, and devoid of realism. Ultimately, the upper limit of a character's intelligence is defined by the upper limit of the screenwriter’s intelligence; a writer simply cannot write a character smarter than themselves. Judging by the original characters in this show, the screenwriters' intellectual ceiling is painfully obvious. And it isn’t just the original characters who got dumbed down—the canon characters suffered a drop in IQ as well. For example, the book’s Wu Sansheng is a shrewd, deeply calculating veteran of the trade, yet the show turns him into a quirky, *tsundere* "moe uncle." A script or novel featuring numerous experts and scholars is notoriously difficult to write well. Experts and scholars represent the intellectual elite of society, possessing intelligence and erudition far beyond the average person. For a screenwriter to give these characters genuine depth and authenticity, the writer must possess an expert-level grasp of the subject matter—which is usually impossible (let alone for certain entertainment-industry writers whose own cultural literacy leaves much to be desired). This is precisely why great shows featuring intellectual elites hire professional consultants. For instance, *The Big Bang Theory* brought on David Saltzberg, a professor of physics and astronomy at UCLA, as a science consultant; meanwhile, one of its executive producers and writers, Eric Kaplan, studied at Harvard, Columbia, and UC Berkeley. It is also worth noting that Mayim Hoya Bialik, who plays Amy Farrah Fowler, holds an actual Ph.D. from UCLA in neuroscience—the exact same field as her character. While some of the characters in that show can act goofy, their intellectual "flexing" holds up to scrutiny. If *The Lost Tomb* production team genuinely wanted to make an "Archaeologists' Chronicles," they should have at least hired a few actual archaeology experts as consultants. As it stands, the show’s level of archaeological expertise cannot even hold a candle to a standard domestic documentary.

**2. Plot Logic**

　　 Many plot details in this show fall apart under the slightest scrutiny, and certain setups display a shocking lack of basic common sense. For example: since High Shao and Wu Xie are supposedly dedicated to protecting cultural relics—and already have prior experience doing so—they should surely know that safeguarding an artifact like the bronze ox head requires the assistance of professional insiders (the preservation, transportation, and security of relics require specialized teams). Instead, these two reckless knuckleheads just toss the artifact into their car and drive straight to Inner Mongolia. This isn’t *protecting* cultural relics; it’s *destroying* them! To make matters worse, Wu Xie actually panics and hurls the priceless ox head off a cliff. So much for "protecting national treasures," huh? Furthermore, as a self-proclaimed "relic protection squad," they ought to know at least a few experts in the field. Yet, upon acquiring the Warring States Silk Scroll, not only do they fail to consult any expert friends, but High Shao actually goes ahead and *posts pictures of the scroll on the internet*. Are they not the least bit worried about being tracked down and robbed by bad actors? This behavior is beyond naive; it is aggressively amateurish. If you're going to make a show about protecting national heritage, could you at least pretend to have some professional ethics? Consequently, the hopelessly unprofessional Wu Xie and High Shao devolve from noble patriotic youths into pure clowns. Comic relief characters are fine and can add flavor to a show, but *The Lost Tomb* is supposed to be a suspense thriller, not a sitcom. What is the point of turning the protagonists into buffoons? Or take the car explosion scene (in Pilot Part 1), which happens for absolutely no discernible reason. Putting aside the physics of whether a single lighter can cause an empty fuel tank to detonate, what was even the narrative point of blowing the car up? Was it just an excuse to show off those laughably cheap explosion CGI effects? On top of that, the mysteriously fluctuating headcount of the mercenaries (perhaps a sloppy editing error) is truly mind-boggling.

**III. Cinematography**

　　 For the most part, the camerawork in this series is aggressively run-of-the-mill; there isn’t a single shot that is uniquely crafted, visually arresting, or jaw-dropping. The framing of certain shots is clumsy, the location scouting feels lazy, and many of the backgrounds are overly cluttered (take the set design of Wu Xie's house, for example), to the point where the frame completely lacks a visual focal point. The show features quite a few landscape shots (such as those around the 3-minute mark of Episode 1), and from a landscape photography standpoint, they fall severely short—some shots even feature tilted horizons, paired with thoroughly washed-out colors.

　　 Some readers might argue: *“Can’t landscape photography feature tilted horizons?”* Allow me to offer my two cents on this: standard photographic composition relies on fundamental rules, such as the Golden Ratio or the Rule of Thirds—principles that apply equally to classical painting. And where did these rules come from? They are the product of centuries of collective human experience; long before photography was invented, master painters codified them. If you look at top-tier modern landscape photography (the kind you see flipping through *National Geographic* or watching BBC and Discovery Channel documentaries) and compare it to the works of old-master landscape painters like Claude Lorrain’s "ideal landscapes," the underlying composition principles are virtually identical. Why? Because these principles inherently align with human visual aesthetics. A cinematographer is certainly free to break the mold and shoot unconventional angles, but without a compelling narrative reason (e.g., to convey disorientation, dynamic motion, or psychological tension), *why* do it? I have no idea what the cinematographer was thinking with the specific shots I highlight below; there was zero narrative justification to abandon basic composition rules there. Because those frames ignore foundational composition, they look inherently awkward. This awkwardness creates a subtle visual friction, and when a show continuously bombards the viewer with that friction, the viewing experience as a whole becomes deeply uncomfortable.

　　 Mastering composition elevates imagery into art. Take Tarsem Singh’s *The Cell*, for instance: the vast majority of its shots strictly adhere to classical composition techniques—most notably the Golden Ratio—and its set design and styling demonstrate a profound artistic foundation. The result is a film that is visually intoxicating and an absolute feast for the eyes. Folks studying experimental film might scoff at my somewhat traditionalist stance on composition, but look at mainstream commercial cinema: time-tested composition methods are still the industry standard (you don't see wonky, tilted landscape framing in sweeping epics like *The Hobbit*). If a filmmaker lacks transcendent technical genius, playing it safe with classical composition rules is always the wiser bet. In the case of *The Lost Tomb*, its wonky framing serves as little purpose as Zhang Qiling’s right-eye-covering side bangs—it adds nothing to the story and serves only to annoy the audience. People watch television to be entertained; what is the point of serving them visually jarring frames that actively sabotage the aesthetic experience?

　　 Composition, tone, focal point establishment—all of these elements dictate the aesthetic value of a frame. When a production drops the ball on any of them, the visuals will feel inherently off. The problem with the specific shots I break down below is that they fail to harmonize these basic elements, severely compromising the aesthetic quality of the frame.

**IV. Visual Effects**

　　 Given that the grave-robbing genre relies heavily on fantastical architecture, treacherous natural environments, and supernatural monsters, the quality of visual effects largely makes or breaks the production. Whether due to a tight budget or a simple lack of effort, the CGI in this show is a massive letdown. But subpar rendering is one thing—what's truly unforgivable is that some of the visual effect *concepts* themselves are profoundly brainless.

**V. Styling**

　　 The character styling in this show certainly has "personality," but it tries way too hard. For example: why on earth is Wu Xie’s hair bleached that obnoxious shade of yellow? It does absolutely nothing to reinforce his character background (whether as the antique shop owner from the novel or the "patriotic overseas student" of the web series); instead, it just gives the protagonist the cheap, tacky vibe of a third-tier hair salon model. Thankfully, his hair color tones down to something slightly more normal in his later scenes. Furthermore, just how much hair gel did they slather onto Zhang Qiling’s head? Why would a stoic, weathered veteran tomb robber (even if he looks young) deliberately visit a salon to get his hair styled like a 2000s boyband member?

**VI. Sound Recording**

　　 When I watched the scene of Wu Xie and High Shao speeding down the road and heard their terribly mismatched dubbing, I thought to myself: *"We're screwed. This damn show is 100% garbage."* Compared to visual elements, audio is much easier for audiences to overlook, but for any production striving for greatness, sound recording is an indispensable craft that demands absolute precision. This show utilized ADR (Automated Dialogue Replacement / post-sync dubbing), as opposed to live sync-sound. A film crew genuinely dedicated to perfection avoids ADR whenever possible, because only live sync-sound can accurately capture the authentic acoustics of a space. Relying on post-dubbing frequently results in flat audio devoid of spatial depth, glaring lip-sync issues, and other amateurish flaws that severely cheapen a production. 

　　 If ADR has so many glaring defects, why do productions still use it? It comes down to budget and stamina. Recording live sync-sound requires the ironclad discipline of the entire cast and crew: the director commands the floor, the script supervisor logs the audio/visual slates, the sound mixer monitors the levels, and the boom operator holds a grueling physical pose to keep the mic in the sweet spot. The moment the director yells *"Action!"*, the entire set must hold its collective breath in seamless cooperation; a mistake by any single person ruins the take. Therefore, one flawless take of live sync-sound is usually built upon the graveyard of dozens of failed takes. Years ago, some friends and I stayed up until midnight agonizing over just a handful of shots, so I know firsthand how exhausting the process is. Sync-sound is tedious and grueling, which is why certain productions cut corners and opt for post-dubbing to save time and money. However, even ADR doesn't have to sound *this* robotic if handled with basic care (the dialogue here lacks any sense of acoustic space, floating awkwardly over the mix and completely killing the atmosphere). In short, from an audio perspective, this show is an undeniably shoddy rush job.

**VIII. Conclusion**

　　As demonstrated above, *The Lost Tomb* web series is riddled with fundamental production flaws. So, why did this show turn out to be such a disaster? The biggest culprit is a staggering lack of sincerity. The producers clearly never treated this project as a piece of art; they merely went through the motions to check off a corporate assignment, bamboozle the fanbase, and rake in some quick cash (seriously, Red Bull—how much product placement money did you pump into this?). When a production operates with zero patience and zero sincerity, the inevitable result is cheap, soulless garbage.

　　In May 2026, Fields Medalist and mathematician Timothy Gowers used ChatGPT 5.5 Pro to solve an open mathematical problem and rewrote the proof process into a complete academic preprint. Gowers noted that in less than an hour, ChatGPT outputted ideas that would have taken him a week or two to conceptualize [1]. Gowers predicted that by 2029, mathematical research could undergo a "seismic shift."

　　Gowers' experience is a microcosm of AI devaluing human labor.

　　Next, I will discuss some phenomena regarding how AI has gradually depreciated the value of human labor during its development in recent years, as well as its psychological impact.

　　Let me first provide a few industry cases:

　　The first case is the impact of AI on the art industry. In recent years, the birth and application of AI painting have become a hot topic in the art field. AI painting has had a profound impact on the industry; image generation models can produce artwork at an incredibly fast speed that would otherwise take human artists a long time to complete. This clearly devalues the labor of artists. The report *Brave New World? Justice for creators in the age of GenAI* released by the Association of Illustrators (AOI), which surveyed 6,844 illustrators, showed that about 1/3 (32%) of illustrators reported losing commissions or having projects canceled due to generative AI (GenAI). The average income loss for these affected illustrators was approximately £9,262 [2].

　　In August 2022, the open-source Stable Diffusion model was released. At that time, AI-generated images had many flaws, including hard-to-control details, messy backgrounds, and deformed hands. Because of these limitations, a new profession was born: the AI retoucher, whose job was to perfect AI-generated images.

　　The Flux model, which emerged in 2024, brought significant improvements in the realism and logical coherence of generated images, largely solving the problem of AI's inability to draw hands. As AI image generation models were perfected, AI retouchers were forced to shift their career paths once again. The rapid evolution of image generation models requires practitioners who use them to continuously learn new knowledge to adapt to the changing trends of the times.

　　The second case is the impact of AI on knowledge providers on social networks. On social media platforms such as X, Quora, Zhihu, and WeChat Official Accounts, there is a group of intellectuals who make a living or find pleasure in providing knowledge. The advent of large language models has disrupted their work and lives:

　　1. Because large language models possess extensive knowledge, the scarcity of human-provided knowledge is greatly diminished. If a knowledge provider relies on monetizing their knowledge to make a living, their income generated from this monetization will decline. This is a severe blow to independent knowledge providers outside the traditional system who rely solely on knowledge monetization for their income. Institutional scholars, because they have fixed salaries, will experience a smaller impact on their income from large language models.

　　2. If a newcomer wants to become a knowledge blogger, lacking a follower base and credibility, they can easily be suspected of using AI for writing if they publish high-quality texts. If the newcomer has works published before the AI era to prove their talent, they might use them to vindicate themselves. However, on social networks, the attention people give to individuals is fleeting; even if someone has pre-AI era works to prove their talent, they might not necessarily get the opportunity to present them.

　　3. Class stratification may become more entrenched. A knowledge provider who established a follower base before the AI era has already built their credibility. Newcomers just entering the industry not only have to compete with their predecessors but also with AI. This may lead to a decrease in the number of newcomers entering the field. With fewer or less competitive newcomers, and veterans already holding secure positions, the hierarchy will solidify.

　　4. The proliferation of AI-generated content triggers a crisis of trust among readers. Before the AI era, high-quality text content on social media was relatively scarce. If someone published a high-quality, long-form text, readers had little reason to suspect it wasn't written by the author themselves, and they were more likely to show appreciation. Today, with the flood of AI-generated content, if someone publishes a high-quality, long-form text, readers will first suspect whether it was written by AI.

　　5. The proliferation of AI-generated content leads to aesthetic fatigue. Before the AI era, long-form, high-quality content on social media was relatively rare, and it would give people a sense of novelty. Today, due to the overabundance of AI-generated long-form high-quality content (such as AI-generated in-depth investigations), the novelty brought by such content has diminished. The rarity of creators producing long-form, high-quality content has decreased, thereby devaluing their labor.

　　The study *Generative AI and news report 2025: How people think about AI’s role in journalism and society* reveals the public's acceptance regarding AI's involvement in news content generation: "There is a clear ‘comfort gap’ between AI- and human-led news. On average, only 12% are comfortable with news made entirely by AI; this rises to 21% with a ‘human in the loop’, 43% when a human leads with some AI help, and 62% for entirely human-made news (an increase of 4 percentage points since 2024). This gap can be found across demographics and countries [3]."

　　Anthropic's 2026 report, *Labor market impacts of AI: A new measure and early evidence*, analyzes AI's actual impact on the job market by measuring its "Observed Exposure"—particularly that of large language models—in real-world work scenarios. The study found that the hiring rate of young adults (aged 22-25) in highly exposed occupations has slowed down. This indicates that while AI may not have caused massive layoffs yet, it could be hindering new laborers from entering these fields.

　　This study also confirms my earlier speculation regarding the difficulties newcomers face in entering the knowledge-providing industry, a trend we must remain vigilant about. Because the application scenarios of large language models are currently limited, highly exposed occupations are confined to certain high-risk roles such as computer programmers, customer service representatives, and data entry clerks. However, in the long run, as AI's performance strengthens, its varieties increase, and its applications become more widespread (such as the widespread use of robotics), people in more professions will be affected. The employment difficulties faced by new laborers will dampen their enthusiasm for learning during their education.

　　The financial hardships caused by unemployment or the inability to find work can be addressed through wealth redistribution (such as a universal unconditional basic income). However, the diminished sense of self-worth resulting from the devaluation of labor cannot be compensated for with money, which could lead to widespread psychological issues.

　　AI sometimes narrows the value gap between different types of labor and reduces the value of learning. To give a specific example:

　　An artist has studied painting for six years. He is just beginning to support himself through his art. Because he is not yet famous and his technique hasn't reached a masterful level, his market recognition is still relatively low. The emergence and gradual maturation of AI image generation technology have severely impacted his work, mainly reflected in the drop in the unit price of his artwork. To secure more market orders to offset this price drop, he uses AI-assisted painting to make up for his lack of production capacity. However, even with AI assistance, the quality of his output is not significantly different from that of an artist who has studied painting for only three years using the same AI assistance. In other words, the gap between the value of six years of hard work and three years of hard work has narrowed.

　　As AI grows stronger, the narrowing value gap between different labor forces and the decreasing value of learning will lead to a problem: enthusiasm for learning will take a hit. The neurological basis for this phenomenon lies in the fact that one of dopamine's core functions is processing Reward Prediction Error (RPE) [4]. This means that the release of dopamine does not depend on how much reward an individual receives, but on whether the received reward exceeds expectations. If the expectations brought about by learning diminish, dopamine secretion will also decrease, thereby lowering our psychological motivation.

　　In the *Exhortation to Learning* (Quan Xue Shi), there is a line encouraging people to study hard through reading: "In books, one finds houses of gold; in books, one finds thousands of bushels of millet; in books, one finds women with faces like jade." This phrase illustrates the benefits of learning through reading. As we discussed earlier, a trend has already emerged: a slowdown in the hiring rate of young adults in highly exposed occupations. In the future era of superintelligence, an extreme scenario might unfold: superintelligence becomes capable of handling most existing jobs, and the new jobs created by AI are far fewer than those it replaces. Generally speaking, by then, most jobs in regions where superintelligence is widely used will no longer require humans, making it impossible for the majority of people to secure employment through learning. Landing a job and achieving a better life through learning is a common driving force for human education; the absence of this motivation will cause a general decline in humanity's overall enthusiasm for learning.

　　We know that happiness often stems from the process of pursuing goals. One of the key neurotransmitters for maintaining human mood is dopamine. When an individual generates expectations or mental representations of positive outcomes (such as potential rewards or goal achievement), dopamine neurons in the midbrain's ventral tegmental area are activated. They release dopamine into the nucleus accumbens, thereby attributing incentive salience to related cues, and creating a motivational drive of "wanting" or deeming something "worth pursuing" [5].

　　If AI replaces human labor on a massive scale and directly provides people with life's necessities, it will significantly eliminate a major category of human goal-pursuit processes—the process of acquiring life's necessities through hard work. The importance of this pursuit process lies not only in the fact that our dopamine generates positive attitudes and joyful emotions during the pursuit, but also because the existence of this process is the very source of our self-worth.

　　For example:

　　A chef who loves cooking and showcases their culinary skills online derives pleasure not only from producing a delicious dish but from enjoying the process and the recognition they receive from others for making it. If AI directly prepares their desired dish for them, they lose not only the joy of the cooking process but also the recognition they would have gained from their labor.

　　An industry thought leader derives pleasure from publishing influential remarks. On one hand, the process of writing and thinking itself brings joy; on the other hand, their statements are valued and praised by followers. If AGI can write a blog post mimicking their style in five seconds, their intellectual value drops significantly. Their thoughts are no longer unique, their inherent scarcity decreases, and their sense of self-worth—which is built upon being an irreplaceable thought leader—will also decline.

　　For those who get their dopamine from active striving, this reduction in the process of acquiring life's necessities through labor will lower their dopamine levels, potentially leading to low moods and depression. Let's use gaming as an example to illustrate how superintelligence taking human jobs and issuing unconditional income could lead to boredom:

　　If you play a shop management simulation game where the system directly gives you 10,000 at the start, but you cannot earn money through management—it just gives you a fixed 10,000 every month, regardless of how you run it, your income can't increase, and your shop can't be upgraded. Such a simulation game would quickly become boring. If you play an RPG game and the moment you log in, you are automatically leveled up to 50 (out of a max level 99), and the system gives you fixed gold coins daily, but you can't level up by fighting monsters, and monsters don't drop gold—would you find such a game fun?

　　The more ambitious a person is, the higher their social status, and the more they derive self-esteem from that status, the harder their expectations for the future will crash when superintelligence crushes their abilities and takes their jobs. Conversely, those who lack ambition, are lazy, and are accustomed to relying on others for support will be much better able to adapt to a life of eating, drinking, and playing on an unconditional basic income.

　　From the perspective of evolutionary psychology, one of the criteria for human mate selection is choosing partners who contribute to the survival of offspring. For example, a common mate selection psychology among women is choosing men with wealth and status. When superintelligence handles human survival (doing work for humans and giving humans money), personal qualities and abilities will no longer be crucial factors in determining the survival quality of offspring. Consequently, the advantage of personal qualities and abilities in mate selection will decrease. Over time, humans might suffer from Domestication Syndrome (domesticated animals often exhibit reduced brain capacity, amygdala shrinkage, and neoteny).

　　Suppose that in the era of superintelligence, most humans lose their jobs or work very little, achieving a worry-free life through an unconditional basic income. To prevent humans from developing domestication syndrome and to allow those who need to find meaning in life through personal labor, effort, and struggle to do so, we can protect the "spark of humanity" by establishing restricted-AI zones. Here are two forms these restricted-AI zones could take:

　　1. **Human Autonomous Zones (Physical):** These autonomous zones are separated from areas where superintelligence is widely used through physical space blockades, and to some extent, they could be called "wild human reserves." Human autonomous zones would be distributed worldwide and connected via local area networks. In these zones, the use of AI is restricted to a level that does not strip humans of their dignity and value. Community members gain a sense of worth through labor. In these communities, professions largely replaced by AI in the superintelligence era—such as lawyers, writers, and painters—would still be entirely held by humans. For the feasibility of such communities, we can look to the Amish. The Amish are a branch of the Anabaptist Christian Protestant group, primarily living in the US and Canada. Due to their religious beliefs, the Amish actively choose to live an agricultural lifestyle akin to the Middle Ages or early 19th century, rejecting most modern technology. Human autonomous zones would not reject modern technology, but they would strictly limit the use of AI.

　　2. **Human Autonomous Virtual Communities:** These restricted-AI communities are deployed on the internet, while the physical living areas of the community participants do not restrict the use of superintelligence. The virtual community needs to use technological means to prevent participants from using superintelligence to cheat and gain unfair advantages in virtual community activities. Since humans in the superintelligence zones do not need to work (or work very little) and receive an unconditional basic income, they can spend a lot of time in the virtual community. The virtual community provides incentive mechanisms to stimulate human competitiveness, such as using the metaverse or massive online games to allow humans to gain status, rankings, and income within the virtual space through their own thinking and effort. Human autonomous virtual communities are designed to prevent humans from becoming bored or losing their meaning in life after losing their jobs and having a worry-free existence. In zoology, there is a concept called "Enrichment." Enrichment refers to the systematic technical practice of using various stimuli in captive conditions to encourage wild animals to exhibit natural behaviors and improve their psychological well-being. To some extent, the "human autonomous virtual community" is an enrichment technology provided to humans after superintelligence "rules" humanity.

　　Generally speaking, the appealing factors of a film can be roughly categorized into the following three types:

　　1. Intense sensory stimulation, such as highly attractive characters, spectacular special effects, eye-catching shots, and pleasing music.
　　2. Engaging plots, such as suspenseful setups and unexpected twists.
　　3. Factors that stimulate positive psychological activity in the audience, such as touching storylines, relatable characters, and thought-provoking themes.

　　Obviously, this film does not focus on creating the first two factors. As a movie involving the sexual abuse of underage girls and the theme of a teenage girl's coming-of-age, the plot does carry some suspense. For instance, when the protagonist, Xiaomi, captures evidence of President Liu sexually abusing a young girl, it naturally makes the audience wonder how she will handle this evidence next. However, compared to outstanding suspense films, the plot of this movie is somewhat flat. While suspense is not the central theme, it would not have been a bad thing if the creators had polished the plot to make the film more exciting and engaging. This reminds me of the Iranian film *A Separation*, another low-budget production featuring ordinary people that reflects class conflict. Through suspenseful plot design and unexpected twists, *A Separation* not only profoundly presents the thought-provoking theme of barriers and conflicts between different classes, but also delivers a compelling and memorable storyline.

　　Meanwhile, the helplessness of ordinary people facing the oppression of power and their unfortunate lives is a factor that resonates well with the audience. A particularly shocking and memorable plot point in the movie is that the female protagonist, Xiaomi, is an undocumented resident (a "black household"). To get the money to apply for an ID card, she blackmails President Liu with the evidence she possesses instead of using it to help the assaulted young girl, Meng Xiaowen. Xiaomi is not a bad person; a life without anyone to rely on has forged her stubbornness. She desires an identity so desperately that breaking the law to obtain it is a desperate measure. Ultimately, however, her conscience drives her to choose justice and fairness. Extreme evil or absolute selflessness are rare in reality; multifaceted characters with genuine human flaws and temperaments, like Xiaomi, are the norm in real life.

　　Throughout the film, the statue of Marilyn Monroe is a recurring motif. What is the symbolic meaning of this statue? During the Q&A session of the filmmaker meet-and-greet held at the Golden Elephant Cinema, the director explained that the statue was inspired by a real news event she happened to come across while writing the screenplay. She views Marilyn Monroe as a representative of objectified women. If so, can the dismantling of the Marilyn Monroe statue be interpreted as a symbol of female awakening? The film ends with a scene where the female protagonist, Xiaomi, wearing a white dress, smashes the chain locking her motorcycle and rides away from her place of exploitation. As she rides, she passes by a truck carrying the dismantled statue of Marilyn Monroe. Does such a scene imply Xiaomi's self-awakening and her pursuit of freedom?

　　In addition, I have always felt that the title *Carnival* lacks a logical connection to the film's content. Regarding the choice of this title, the director explained that she chose it because she hoped people would pay attention to the darkness behind the beautiful and glamorous facade. While the director's intention is commendable, there is nothing in the film's content that relates to a carnival, making the choice of this title feel somewhat forced.
